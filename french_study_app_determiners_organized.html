Core subject pronouns

These are the pronouns you combine with verbs like être and avoir.

Être — to be

Use être for identity, state, nationality, location, and descriptions.

Avoir — to have

Use avoir for possession, age, and many common expressions.

Mini pattern notes

Je becomes j’ before vowel sounds: j’ai, j’habite.

Tu is informal “you.” Vous is formal singular or plural “you.”

On often means everyday spoken “we,” but it still takes singular verb forms: on est, on a.

Ils is “they” for a masculine or mixed group. Elles is “they” for an all-female group.

How this tab is organized

Possessive mon/ma/mes, ton/ta/tes, etc.

Definite articles

Use these when you mean the. Choose by gender, number, and whether the next word begins with a vowel sound.

Indefinite articles

Use these for a / an / some with countable nouns.

Partitive articles

Use these for some with things like food, drinks, and other uncountable nouns.

Demonstrative determiners

Use these for this / that / these / those. cet appears before a masculine singular noun with a vowel sound.

Possessive determiners

These agree with the noun being possessed, not with the owner. Click any form to hear it with an example.

Vowel-sound tip: before a feminine singular noun starting with a vowel sound, French often uses mon / ton / son: mon amie, ton idée, son école.
